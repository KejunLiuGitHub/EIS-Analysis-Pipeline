Abstract. EIS analysis of a gel polymer electrolyte (GPE) thin film in symmetric SS | GPE | SS blocking-electrode configuration, 7 MHz → 1 Hz. Data cleaning removed 16 inductive artifact points (f > 216 kHz) caused by cable parasitic inductance. The best-fitting equivalent circuit model is R0 + (R1 ‖ CPE1) + (R2 ‖ CPE2) with χ²/(N−p) = 23.0. Fitted parameters: R0 = 20.2 Ω (contact), R1 = 653 Ω (bulk ionic transport, fc,1 = 46 Hz), R2 = 3455 Ω (electrode interface, fc,2 = 0.13 Hz). The ionic conductivity from R1 is σ = 5.85 × 10−6 S·cm−1 (L = 30 μm, A = 0.785 cm2). Harmonic distortion analysis reveals severe nonlinearity (THD I > 10% at 18/69 frequencies) caused by the excessive 100 mV AC amplitude, explaining the appearance of the second arc at the nominally blocking electrode interface. 2. Data Cleaning & Fitting

2.1 Inductive Artifact Removal

At f > 216 kHz, Im(Z) > 0 (inductive, 4th quadrant in Nyquist). This is caused by parasitic cable inductance (ZL = jωL), which dominates at MHz frequencies. The 16 inductive points were removed. The remaining 53 points (171.6 kHz → 1 Hz) constitute the capacitive SPE response.

The frequency where Im(Z) = 0 is fcross = 171.6 kHz, with Re(Z(fcross)) = 29.74 Ω. This is identified as R0 + residual bulk tail, not the full bulk resistance Rb (see §3).

3. Equivalent Circuit Model & Mathematics

3.1 Element Impedances

$$Z_R = R \qquad Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} \qquad Z_{\mathrm{CPE}} = \frac{1}{Q\,(j\omega)^\alpha}, \quad 0 \leq \alpha \leq 1$$

Using $j^\alpha = e^{j\alpha\pi/2} = \cos(\alpha\pi/2) + j\sin(\alpha\pi/2)$:

$$\Re(Z_{\mathrm{CPE}}) = \frac{\cos(\alpha\pi/2)}{Q\,\omega^\alpha}, \qquad -\Im(Z_{\mathrm{CPE}}) = \frac{\sin(\alpha\pi/2)}{Q\,\omega^\alpha}$$

The exponent $\alpha$ characterizes the deviation from ideal behavior: $\alpha=1$ → ideal capacitor ($Q \equiv C$); $\alpha=0$ → ideal resistor ($Q \equiv 1/R$); $\alpha=0.5$ → Warburg diffusion.

3.2 $R \parallel \mathrm{CPE}$ (ZARC Element)

$$Z_{R\parallel\mathrm{CPE}} = \frac{R}{1 + R Q (j\omega)^\alpha}$$

Explicit real–imaginary decomposition (multiplying numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate):

$$\Re = \frac{R\,[1 + R Q \omega^\alpha\cos(\alpha\pi/2)]}{1 + 2R Q \omega^\alpha\cos(\alpha\pi/2) + R^2 Q^2 \omega^{2\alpha}}$$ $$-\Im = \frac{R^2 Q \omega^\alpha\sin(\alpha\pi/2)}{1 + 2R Q \omega^\alpha\cos(\alpha\pi/2) + R^2 Q^2 \omega^{2\alpha}}$$

The effective capacitance of a CPE in parallel with $R$ [Brug et al., 1984]:

$$C_{\mathrm{eff}} = Q^{1/\alpha}\, R^{(1-\alpha)/\alpha}$$

Characteristic (peak) frequency:

$$\omega_c = \frac{1}{R\,C_{\mathrm{eff}}} = \frac{1}{R^{1/\alpha}\,Q^{1/\alpha}}, \qquad f_c = \frac{\omega_c}{2\pi}$$

3.3 R(RQ)(RQ) — Two ZARC Elements in Series

$$Z_{\mathrm{total}}(\omega) = R_0 + \frac{R_1}{1 + R_1 Q_1 (j\omega)^{\alpha_1}} + \frac{R_2}{1 + R_2 Q_2 (j\omega)^{\alpha_2}}$$

Physical Interpretation of the Two Arcs

  1. Arc 1 ($f_{c,1} = 46$ Hz, $C_{\mathrm{eff},1} = 5.3$ μF): Bulk ionic transport through the GPE. The depressed shape ($\alpha_1 = 0.68$) reflects the distribution of relaxation times in the heterogeneous polymer matrix (chain segment motion, ion hopping between coordination sites).
  2. Arc 2 ($f_{c,2} = 0.13$ Hz, $C_{\mathrm{eff},2} = 361$ μF): Electrode interface process. The very large effective capacitance (460 μF/cm²) and low characteristic frequency are consistent with an electrochemical charge-transfer reaction at the SS electrode, induced by the 100 mV overpotential. Under proper 10 mV linear excitation, this feature is expected to revert to a capacitive blocking tail ($\mathrm{CPE}_{dl}$).

Why the Blocking-Electrode Model Failed

The classical SPE model $R_0 + (R_b \parallel \mathrm{CPE}_b) + \mathrm{CPE}_{dl}$ gave $\chi^2/N > 2000$. Two factors explain this:

  1. The 100 mV amplitude induces weak Faradaic reactions at the SS electrode, transforming the purely capacitive blocking tail into a resistive-capacitive arc with finite $R_2$.
  2. Even if the interface were perfectly blocking, the CPE exponent $\alpha_{dl} \approx 0.3$ would create a strongly inclined line that, when only measured to 1 Hz (above $f_{c,2}$), resembles the onset of a second arc.

4. Harmonic Distortion Analysis

The BioLogic instrument records the total harmonic distortion (THD) and individual harmonic amplitudes ($h_2$–$h_7$) for both the applied voltage ($E_{\mathrm{we}}$) and the measured current ($I$) at every frequency. These data, available in the .mpr file but absent from the exported .txt, provide direct evidence of nonlinearity caused by the 100 mV amplitude.

4.2 DC Offset

The instrument records $\langle E_{\mathrm{we}}\rangle = 0.425$ V at all frequencies, despite the setpoint being 0 V. The mean current $\langle I\rangle$ is consistently negative (−0.001 to −0.009 mA), indicating a small cathodic (reduction) current flowing at the SS electrode. This confirms that the electrode is not perfectly blocking under the measurement conditions.

Conclusion: The THD and DC offset data independently confirm that the 100 mV AC amplitude drives the system into a nonlinear regime at low frequencies. Arc 2 ($R_2 = 3455$ Ω, $f_c = 0.13$ Hz) is interpreted as a nonlinear interfacial charge-transfer process, not an intrinsic property of the GPE. Under proper 10 mV excitation, this arc is expected to disappear, and the classical blocking-electrode model $R_0 + (R_b \parallel \mathrm{CPE}_b) + \mathrm{CPE}_{dl}$ should become applicable.

6. Error Analysis

  1. 100 mV AC amplitude (largest error source). THD I exceeds 10% at 18 frequencies and reaches 20% at 1 Hz. This violates the linearity condition required for valid EIS analysis [Orazem & Tribollet, 2017]. The nonlinear response creates Arc 2 and may reduce the apparent $R_1$. True linear $R_b$ could be 10–30% larger than the measured $R_1 = 653$ Ω.
  2. Incomplete Arc 2. The lowest measured frequency (1 Hz) is above $f_{c,2} = 0.13$ Hz. Arc 2 is only partially resolved — $R_2$ and $\alpha_2$ should be treated as approximate. Extending the measurement to 0.01 Hz would fully characterize this feature.
  3. Cable artifact. The 16 removed inductive points ($f > 216$ kHz) contain the high-frequency side of Arc 1. The fitted $R_0 = 20.2$ Ω is an extrapolation. The true high-frequency intercept is not directly measured. Shorter, shielded cables would push the inductive artifact above 1 MHz.
  4. Film thickness. At 30 μm, ±5 μm variation propagates to ±17% uncertainty in $\sigma$. The reported value assumes uniform thickness.
  5. Temperature. Not recorded. SPE conductivity follows Arrhenius or VFT temperature dependence. The reported $\sigma$ is valid only at the (unknown) measurement temperature.
  6. Zero-crossing misinterpretation. When the bulk characteristic frequency lies above the cable artifact cutoff, the Im($Z$) = 0 crossing conflates $R_0$ with the residual bulk arc tail, systematically underestimating $R_b$.

7. Recommendations for Future Experiments

Based on the findings of this analysis, the following measurements are recommended to obtain publication-quality data:

7.1 Immediate Corrections (Next Measurement)

  1. Reduce AC amplitude to 10 mV. This is the single most important change. At 10 mV, the system should remain in the linear regime (THD < 1%), and the blocking electrode interface should revert to a capacitive tail ($\mathrm{CPE}_{dl}$) rather than the current pseudo-Faradaic Arc 2.
  2. Extend low-frequency limit to 0.01–0.1 Hz. This will fully resolve the electrode polarization feature. At 10 mV, a near-vertical capacitive tail is expected.
  3. Use shorter, shielded measurement cables to push the inductive artifact above 1 MHz, maximizing the usable bandwidth for resolving the bulk semicircle.
  4. Record the measurement temperature. Even an approximate value (±1 °C) is essential for comparing $\sigma$ with literature values.

7.2 Controlled Variable-Temperature Study

  1. Measure EIS at 5–8 temperatures spanning 25–80 °C. Thermally equilibrate for ≥30 min at each temperature before measurement.
  2. Fit the temperature-dependent conductivity to the Arrhenius equation: $$\sigma(T) = \sigma_0 \exp\left(-\frac{E_a}{k_B T}\right)$$ or the VFT equation for cooperative ion transport: $$\sigma(T) = \frac{A}{\sqrt{T}} \exp\left(-\frac{B}{T - T_0}\right)$$ The activation energy $E_a$ is a key metric for comparing electrolytes and understanding the ion transport mechanism.

7.3 Complementary Characterization

  1. Linear Sweep Voltammetry (LSV) at 1–10 mV/s to determine the electrochemical stability window of the electrolyte.
  2. Chronoamperometry (DC polarization) with the same SS | GPE | SS cell to independently measure the electronic conductivity and confirm predominantly ionic conduction (electronic transference number $t_e \ll 1$).
  3. Verify thickness uniformity by SEM cross-section or micrometer measurements at multiple points across the film.

7.4 Data Analysis Protocol

  1. Always read the .mpr file directly (via galvani) rather than relying on .txt exports. The 34-column .mpr dataset includes THD and harmonic data essential for verifying measurement validity.
  2. Check THD before fitting. If THD I > 5% at any frequency, flag the measurement as potentially nonlinear and either exclude those frequencies or discuss the nonlinearity explicitly.
  3. Cross-validate $R_b$ using both the zero-crossing method and full model fitting. If the two disagree by more than 20%, the zero-crossing is likely contaminated by cable artifact or contact resistance.
